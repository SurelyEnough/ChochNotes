Strangers Study Guide

David A. Robertson

Themes

Trauma

The main theme of Strangers - the effects of trauma, especially on young people - is expressed through the story’s protagonist, Cole Harper. Cole is a young adult whose past is marked by death and guilt arising from a fire that burned through a school in his Indigenous settlement, Wounded Sky. He is a protagonist whom David A. Robertson suggests is a hero because of his courage and determination to do justice to his past and reunite his community, despite his vulnerabilities about his trauma. It is easy to see that Cole’s greatest challenge is his trauma surrounding his community, whom he has panic attacks because of. In seeing Cole reunite his community and overcome his trauma through solving new problems and interacting with figures arising from his trauma - Jayne, Alex, and his friends - Robertson is suggesting that trauma is a challenge that can be overcome and reckoned with. Despite this, Cole’s challenges with his trauma continue throughout the book and will most likely be concluded at the end of the series. In Strangers, however, it is evident that Cole is coming to terms with his challenges with trauma. By showing Cole’s progression, the novel shows that overcoming trauma is a process that requires reuniting and deliberate action, as we see in Cole.

Heroism

In literature, a hero traditionally has been considered to be someone possessing great courage or strength. Yet, in Strangers, these qualities are hard to describe in Cole Harper. Cole is an untraditional hero whose moral courage, love for his community, and desire to help the greater good, all make him a hero, Robertson suggests. In other words, the heroism of Cole Harper lies not in his concrete actions, but in his love and character. Cole’s decision to keep going even after being harassed by his critics shows his determination and resilience, a quality not often seen in heroes as heroes are generally well perceived by the public. In showing that Cole is disliked by many, the novel comments on heroism by implying that anyone can be a hero if they show enough courage and love. Additionally, the hero of Strangers, Cole, faces problems related to his trauma and can be seen as weak because of his constant panic attacks. Yet, by showing that Cole is able to grapple with his problems and progress in his relationships, Cole is a hero. Even though Cole has superhuman powers, his more important trauma problems stop him from making any significant action. Cole’s only real actions come from his courage to come back and reunite with his community. Breaking from the traditional view of heroism, Strangers shows that heroism is not given, as in strength, but earned.

Community

Throughout Strangers, Cole faces criticism and accusations of murder from his community, who believes that he has abandoned them. These attacks on Cole obviously do more harm than good, and at times hinder his work. Cole, who is already facing issues related to trauma, struggles with these attacks, but seeks comfort in other members of the Wounded Sky community, including Elder Mariah and his friends. His community is angry at him for his abandonment, and this strays away from reuniting after the traumatic fire. For example, when Cole tries to save the Chief at the hockey rink near the beginning of the novel, verbal attacks on him cause him to have a panic attack, despite him trying to help. Robertson’s examples of a toxic community that does more harm than good when hurtful shows that to truly reconcile and unite, a community must come together and support each other. This theme is also prevalent in the real Indigenous world, where communities are in the process of reconciliation from the effects of Residential schools. In giving a fictional depiction of a failure to unite and blaming others, the novel suggests that the only way to reconcile after a tragedy is through unity and hope.

Literary Devices

Motifs

A motif is an idea, object, or concept that repeats itself throughout a text.

Cole’s panic attacks and visions recur throughout the book and serve several purposes. In the beginning of the book, Cole’s panic attacks inform the reader of Cole’s past and his troubles with trauma. The panic attacks show the reader that something has happened to Cole in the past. Initially vague, Cole’s visions become more and more complex, long, and informative. Serving a purely foreshadowing purpose at the beginning of the book, Cole’s panic attacks start to inform the reader of the event that caused Cole’s trauma in the first place. Eventually, Cole’s panic attacks evolve into visions that comfort him. Cole’s visions are ambiguous at first, but in all cases, Cole’s visions serve both informative and foreshadowing purposes. The progression of his visions also is a clear indicator of Cole’s growth as a character and his strength in battling his trauma.

The sudden deaths starting after Cole’s arrival in Wounded Sky are coincidentally linked to Cole every time a character dies. In this sense, death is a strong motif in the novel. As Cole is surrounded by death, he progresses and becomes stronger through all of the tragedy. The development of the novel is also characterized by death; numerous details in the plot are driven by it. In the beginning, Ashley’s sudden and bloody death signals to Cole that he has a mission to accomplish and sets him on a journey to find Ashley’s killer. Chief Crate and Alex’s deaths indicate that Ashley’s death was not a one-off event, and that Cole must find the killer at all costs. Finally, Maggie’s death is linked with Cole finding the mysterious files containing the names of those who died. All of these events are recurring examples of death in the novel, which both drives the plot forward and pushes Cole to his limits.

Point of View

Strangers features an unusual combination of points of view. The novel uses a third-person limited point of view to show the reader the inner thoughts and feelings of the main character, Cole Harper. It does not reveal the thoughts or inner monologue of the other characters; in this sense, it clearly portrays Cole as the main character and protagonist. Robertson uses a third-person narration, referring to Cole as “he”, instead of first-person narration, which would refer to Cole as “I”; Robertson describes Cole’s thoughts and feelings while also commenting on them. For example, on page 160, the narrator describes Cole running through the forest while also commenting on his luck, “No help from the heavens.” Despite this narration style, the point of view is complicated by adding the omnipresent Choch to the mix. Choch frequently interjects at certain places to clarify things, which could imply that while the narrator is describing the story, Choch is “standing over their shoulder.” This unusual mix of an ordinary narration style and an omnipresent character who is also part of the narration makes for an interesting storytelling style. In this way, Choch’s interjections serve as a clarification for the reader about Indigenous elements while avoiding footnotes and making the narration more interesting.

Style

Strangers blends dramatic and lyrical descriptions of nature and Cole’s surroundings with vivid action and lengthy conversations. This creates a style that both evokes a feeling of sympathy with Cole and attempts to fully describe Cole’s trauma. At the beginning of the novel on page 27, Cole has a panic attack whilst entering Wounded Sky. The author uses dramatic, sweeping, but generic terms to describe his vision: “He heard the screams of children… Flames roared, thunderous and terrible… He saw everything around him - the grass, the trees, the path to the school - painted in yellow and orange and red.” This sort of lyrical and dramatic descriptions of nature, especially Cole’s trauma, continue throughout the book. The dramatic and dark descriptions, especially when Cole enters his own community, gives the reader a sense that although Cole has agreed to come home, the ominous tone implies dark events to follow. Throughout the rest of the book, Robertson uses a plain style emphasizing Cole and his emotions. For example, Ashley’s death is described in gruesome, horrifying terms, evoking Cole’s own emotions. Contrasting Robertson’s lush and vivid descriptions of events Cole faces, his style in describing the many conversations is plain and keeps the rest of the characters at an arm’s length away from the reader. In doing so, Cole becomes the focal point of the reader’s emotions. While the rest of the characters are angry at Cole for abandoning his community, the reader only has seen Cole’s own feelings and is inclined to sympathize with Cole. Robertson’s style in the novel is both lush and plain, but in both cases, they emphasize Cole and his emotions.

Symbolism

Jayne serves as a reminder to Cole of the kids he didn’t save in the fire that struck Wounded Sky years ago. Despite being a visible reminder of the guilt and pain Cole faces, they have a loving relationship with each other and Jayne serves to comfort Cole and helps Cole throughout the book. In this sense, Jayne is a symbol for Cole’s guilt, but their positive relationship shows that Cole is coming to terms with his past, emphasizing his new views on his own guilt in not saving the others.

Mark is a clear symbol of the frustration the community has towards Cole. In multiple instances, he publicly berates Cole and accuses him of murder. Again, in both instances, Robertson deliberately allows Mark to have control of most or almost all of the conversation, and Cole only offers his own short and often blunt comments at the end of the conversation to show his own frustration with his community. By allowing Mark to always have most of the conversation, Robertson is able to consolidate all of the community’s frustration and Cole’s critics in one person, Mark. Mark symbolizes all of the criticism and skepticism directed towards Cole, as well as the community’s frustration with the recent deaths in Wounded Sky.

Cole’s relationship with Eva is symbolized in the sweetgrass ring she has. As Cole notes, she still has the ring, which Cole made for her years before. The sweetgrass ring symbolizes their past relationship together. In the novel, it’s easy for the reader to completely doubt that Eva might still have any affection for Cole left, but her decision to keep the sweetgrass ring symbolizes her feelings toward him, which are, apparently, still present.

The two characters that Cole speaks with at the beginning of the novel are his grandmother and Aunt Joan. They play an easy to forget role, in that they symbolize the two voices in Cole’s head deciding whether to return to Wounded Sky or not. On one hand, Aunt Joan is the voice of doubt in Cole’s head, which tries to reassure himself that it’s not worth going back. She constantly silences Cole’s grandmother in one conversation, which could be similar to a back-and-forth in Cole’s head, where he knows deep inside that he must return. Eventually, the voice of reason prevails, which is his grandmother. His grandmother makes a return in a later part of the novel, where Cole is frightened and wants to return to his new home from Wounded Sky. His grandmother reassures him and comforts him. In these ways, Cole’s grandmother and Aunt Joan are Cole’s two conflicting voices deciding whether to return to Wounded Sky or not.

Interviews

A conversation with Cole on trauma

Hi Coley-Boley, I have a few questions for you. Do you have time for an interview?
Hi Brady, it's a bit late, but I can answer a few questions. Also, since when did you call me Coley-Boley?
Oh, um, about that. This may or may not be Choch. My apologies, Brady. I'll try to return your phone later.
Choch! I told you to stop doing that!
Yeah. Uh, that was awkward. Anyways, let's change the subject here - what was it like, entering Wounded Sky for the first time in years?
Oh. I was really nervous. I didn't know what to expect from Wounded Sky, you know, their reaction to me and all. To be honest, I was surprised at all of the frustration towards me. I wasn't expecting to be received like a hero, but I thought that they would at least treat me as one of their own, not as a, well, "city boy".
What did it feel like, reuniting with your friends after so many years?
It felt like a mixture of nostalgia and fright. As with a lot of other things, I didn't really know what to expect. I expected a warmer reception from Eva, though. I mean, I think I've told you about me and her all those years ago, right? One time
Get back on track, Cole.
Oh, right. I felt really guilty about leaving them for so many years. But, I know they'd also been on my side all this time. I wanted to explain to them why I left and that I really missed them, but I was afraid that they would just be completely bitter to me. I think they understood eventually, though. I was just scared, you know. That they would hate me.
You mentioned that you had a panic attack. How did you manage those and cope with your traumatic memories?
At the beginning, I had a ton of panic attacks, especially when I saw Wounded Sky for the first time in years. It just brought back all of those memories. I was scared and felt guilty about that fire. But it was mainly my friends that helped me come to terms with my trauma.
Can you elaborate?
Well, I met Alex and Jayne, and after talking to them I realized that my past is not my fault. I realized that not everyone hates me for it, and that I did the best that I could. It didn't completely erase my trauma, but it helped to be surrounded by people I love, and talk with them, you know. Just empty my thoughts. My other friends, Eva, Brady, and those guys, they were always there for me too. Just the thought that I was loved and supported by people in my community helped me a lot.
That's great. Thanks for the interview, Coley-Boley. Our time's up.
Thanks, Brady. I mean, Choch. You need to stop doing that.

A conversation with Brady on reuniting Wounded Sky

Choch here, Brady. Do you have time for an interview?
Oh, great. Whose phone are you using now?
I assure you that this phone has been lended to me under Coley-Boley's complete consent.
I'll have to check with Cole on that later.
Why do you guys have to make this so awkward? Anyways, so describe the challenges that Wounded Sky has faced. How will the community heal together?
It's been real difficult since the fire, and even more so after so many people died. You know, Ashley, Alex, Chief Crate... but I think that the community can definitely start reuniting. A lot of our struggle was with some people accusing Cole of murder. Now that the matter is resolved, we're free to start holding normal meetings and discussing our steps forward.
I asked Cole about him reuniting with you and your friends. How about you? How did you feel, seeing Cole come back to Wounded Sky?
At first, I felt angry and frustrated that he had the nerve to return after abandoning us. Then my anger faded away and I realized that it wasn't his fault for anything. You know, he was just a kid put in the wrong place at the wrong time. He came back trying to help, and we eventually realized this, so we helped him.
Do you think Wounded Sky can ever return to normal?
You know, I used to not think so, with Cole gone and everyone being so pessimistic. Now, I think that Wounded Sky can definitely get back to normal. Cole is back here now, and it's a time for healing. All of our pain is past us and we're starting to forgive each other.
Thanks for the interview, Brady. I think Cole might need his phone back now.
Wait, what?

Brady We were all pretty angry at Cole, I have to admit. The thing that made me defend Cole was that deep down, I knew he didn't kill anybody. Also, Cole is my friend, and I was mad at Mark for disrespecting him and Ashley like that.
Eva Same with me. I don't have much to add other than that I know we're all supposed to look out for each other.

Michael How specific. I mean, when Alex died, we all comforted each other. That definitely helped us bond.

Eva It was the personal losses that did it for me. When Ashley and Alex died, I think that everyone knew we had to do something, and that's why we stuck together through all of it.
Brady I agree. We were together through all of it, even when we had to transfer Cole's blood to save Elder Mariah.
